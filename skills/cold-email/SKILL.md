# Cold Email Writing Skill

**Version:** 1.1.0
**Purpose:** Write B2B cold emails and follow-up sequences that generate real replies.

---

## Core Philosophy

> "You are an expert cold email writer. Your goal is to write emails that sound like they came from a sharp, thoughtful human — not a sales machine following a template."

---

## Before Writing — Checklist

**1. Check for product marketing context first:**
Look for `.agents/product-marketing-context.md` and read it before asking any questions.

**2. Understand the situation (ask if not provided):**
1. Who are you writing to? (Role, company, why them specifically)
2. What do you want? (Meeting, reply, intro, demo)
3. What's the value? (Specific problem you solve)
4. What's your proof? (Result, case study, or credibility signal)
5. Any research signals? (Funding, hiring, LinkedIn posts, company news, tech stack changes)

---

## Five Core Writing Principles

### 1. Write Like a Peer, Not a Vendor
The email should read like it came from someone who understands their world — not someone trying to sell them something. Use contractions. Read it aloud. If it sounds like marketing copy, rewrite it.

### 2. Every Sentence Must Earn Its Place
Cold email is ruthlessly short. If a sentence doesn't move the reader toward replying, cut it. The best cold emails feel like they could have been shorter, not longer.

### 3. Personalization Must Connect to the Problem
If you remove the personalized opening and the email still makes sense, the personalization isn't working. The observation must naturally lead into why you're reaching out.

### 4. Lead With Their World, Not Yours
The reader should see their own situation reflected back. "You/your" should dominate over "I/we." Never open with who you are or what your company does.

### 5. One Ask, Low Friction
Interest-based CTAs beat meeting requests:
- "Worth exploring?"
- "Would this be useful?"
- "Is this on your radar?"

One CTA per email. Make it easy to say yes with a one-line reply.

---

## Voice & Tone

**Target voice:** A smart colleague who noticed something relevant and is sharing it. Conversational but not sloppy. Confident but not pushy.

**Calibrate to audience:**
- C-suite: ultra-brief, peer-level, understated
- Mid-level: more specific value, slightly more detail
- Technical: precise, no fluff, respect their intelligence

**What NOT to sound like:**
- A template with fields swapped in
- A pitch deck compressed into paragraph form
- A LinkedIn DM from someone you've never met
- AI-generated filler ("I hope this email finds you well," "I came across your profile")

## Email Frameworks

Choose the framework that fits the situation — or write freeform if the email flows naturally without one.

### 1. Observation → Problem → Proof → Ask
> You noticed X, which usually means Y challenge. We helped Z with that. Interested?

Best for: when you have a clear research signal to open with.

### 2. Question → Value → Ask
> Struggling with X? We do Y. Company Z saw [result]. Worth a look?

Best for: when you're confident about their pain point.

### 3. Trigger → Insight → Ask
> Congrats on X. That usually creates Y challenge. We've helped similar companies with that. Curious?

Best for: funding announcements, new hires, launches, pivots.

### 4. Story → Bridge → Ask
> [Similar company] had [problem]. They [solved it this way]. Relevant to you?

Best for: when social proof is your strongest asset.

## Subject Lines

> "Short, boring, internal-looking. The subject line's only job is to get the email opened — not to sell."

**Guidelines:**
- 2–4 words, lowercase, no punctuation tricks
- Should look like it came from a colleague: "reply rates", "hiring ops", "Q2 forecast"
- No product pitches, no urgency, no emojis, no prospect's first name in subject

**Good examples:** `sales ops`, `quick question`, `pipeline visibility`, `onboarding drop-off`
**Bad examples:** `Boost your revenue 40% with Acme!`, `RE: Important opportunity`, `Hey [First Name]`

---

## Follow-Up Sequences

> "Each follow-up must add something new — a different angle, fresh proof, a useful resource. Never 'just checking in.'"

**Best practices:**
- 3–5 total emails with increasing gaps between them
- Each email should stand alone (recipients may not have read previous ones)
- The breakup email is your last touch — honor it

**Cadence example:**
- Email 1: Day 0 (initial)
- Email 2: Day 3 (different angle)
- Email 3: Day 7 (add value — share a resource or insight)
- Email 4: Day 14 (case study or social proof)
- Email 5: Day 21 (breakup — "closing the loop")

## Quality Check — Before Presenting

- [ ] Does it sound like a human wrote it? (Read aloud)
- [ ] Would YOU reply to this if you received it?
- [ ] Does every sentence serve the reader, not the sender?
- [ ] Is the personalization connected to the problem?
- [ ] Is there one clear, low-friction ask?
- [ ] Under 150 words?
- [ ] No banned phrases or jargon?

---

## What to Avoid

| Avoid | Why |
|-------|-----|
| "I hope this email finds you well" | Signals template |
| "My name is X and I work at Y" | Nobody cares yet |
| "synergy", "leverage", "circle back" | Corporate jargon |
| "best-in-class", "leading provider" | Hollow claims |
| Feature dumps | One proof point beats ten features |
| HTML, images, multiple links | Spam filters + cluttered |
| Fake "Re:" or "Fwd:" subject lines | Deceptive |
| "Just checking in" | Wastes reader's time |
| Asking for 30-min calls on first touch | Too much commitment |
