# Product Marketing Context
## Lockton Companies Singapore — Trade Credit Insurance

---

## 1. Product Overview

- **Product / Company Name:** Lockton Companies Singapore
- **One-liner:** Singapore's specialist trade credit insurance broker — we fight for your claim so you don't have to.
- **What it does:** Lockton places and manages Trade Credit Insurance (TCI) policies for mid-market businesses in Singapore, protecting their accounts receivable against buyer non-payment, insolvency, and protracted default. Unlike going direct to an insurer, Lockton acts as the client's advocate — negotiating credit limits, managing renewals, and fighting their corner at claims time.
- **Product category:** Trade credit insurance broker / credit risk advisory
- **Product type:** Broker / intermediary (independent, not tied to any single insurer)
- **Pricing model:** Broker fee / commission embedded in premium. Premium is typically 0.1%–0.5% of insured turnover depending on sector, buyer concentration, and geography.

---

## 2. Target Audience

- **Target company type:** Singapore-based mid-market companies, $50M–$500M annual revenue, with meaningful B2B receivables exposure — particularly those selling on open account terms domestically or cross-border
- **Decision-maker roles:** CFO, Finance Director, Group Finance Manager, CEO/MD (owner-managed businesses), Credit Manager, Risk Manager, Commercial Director
- **Primary use case:** Protecting accounts receivable from buyer non-payment, enabling safer credit extension to buyers, and unlocking trade finance / bank facilities that require insured receivables
- **Jobs to be done:**
  - Protect cash flow from a single large buyer default that could be existential
  - Reduce concentration risk when too much revenue sits with a handful of buyers
  - Satisfy bank covenants to access trade finance, invoice financing, or revolving credit facilities
  - Expand into new markets (China, India, Middle East, SEA) with confidence in unknown buyer credit quality
  - Replace letters of credit (LoCs) with cheaper, more flexible open account terms backed by insurance

## 4. Problems & Pain Points

- **Core challenge before finding a solution:** Mid-market companies either carry receivables risk naked (and get hurt when a major buyer defaults) or rely on expensive, rigid letters of credit that slow down trade and strain buyer relationships.
- **Why current solutions fall short:**
  - Going direct to Euler Hermes / Coface / Atradius: you get a policy but no independent advocate when credit limits get cut or claims get disputed — the insurer's interest is to minimise payout, not to fight for you
  - Letters of credit: expensive, administratively heavy, and buyers increasingly refuse them — especially in Asia where open account is the norm
  - Self-insuring / bad debt provisions: works on small exposures but a single large default can be catastrophic and is not a real risk transfer
  - Doing nothing: common until it isn't — one major buyer insolvency changes everything fast
- **Financial / opportunity cost:** A single large bad debt in the $500K–$5M range can wipe out 6–12 months of net profit for a mid-market trading or manufacturing company. Beyond the direct loss: banks may reduce facilities, confidence in extending credit to new buyers evaporates, and growth stalls.
- **Emotional tension:** The CFO who knows the top 3 buyers represent 60% of receivables and lies awake wondering "what if." The business owner who had a bad debt three years ago and still carries the scar. The sales director who can't offer competitive credit terms and is losing deals.

## 6. Differentiation

- **Key differentiators:**
  - Independent broker — access to Euler Hermes, Coface, Atradius, Credendo, QBE and others; can structure the best fit across multiple insurers
  - Claims advocacy — when a claim is disputed or a credit limit is cut without explanation, Lockton fights for the client, not the insurer
  - Renewal management — proactive credit limit monitoring so clients aren't blindsided at renewal
  - Singapore and Asia market depth — understanding of buyer credit risk specific to Singapore, China, SEA, India, Middle East trade flows
- **How the problem is solved differently:** Lockton sits on the client's side of the table throughout the policy lifecycle — at placement, at credit limit review, and especially at claims. Clients who go direct to an insurer have no one in their corner when things go wrong.
- **Why customers choose Lockton:** When something goes wrong — a buyer misses payment, a credit limit gets cut, a claim gets queried — Lockton's client has an expert advocate making calls, writing letters, and escalating. Clients who went direct to the insurer are on their own.

## 7. Objections & Anti-Personas

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "We've never had a bad debt" | One large default can wipe out years of profit. TCI isn't bought after the bad debt — it's bought before. |
| "We already have a policy with [Euler Hermes / Coface]" | The question isn't whether you have a policy — it's whether you have someone in your corner when a credit limit gets cut or a claim gets disputed. Going direct means the insurer's team handles your claim. |
| "It's too expensive" | Premium is typically 0.1%–0.5% of insured turnover. Compare that to writing off a single bad debt. |
| "Our buyers are very reliable" | Buyer credit quality changes — funding rounds fail, supply chains break, key markets contract. The risk isn't your relationship with them; it's what happens to them. |
| "We use letters of credit" | LoCs are expensive, buyers increasingly refuse them, and they're not viable for domestic trade. TCI enables open account terms that win more business at lower cost. |

**Who is NOT a good fit:**
- Pure domestic retail / consumer-facing businesses with no significant B2B receivables
- Startups with revenue under $5M
- Companies where all buyers pay in advance or via LoC with no open account exposure
- Companies with a single buyer (concentration too high for most insurers to cover)

## 8. Switching Dynamics (Four Forces)

- **Push** (frustrations driving them away from status quo): A recent bad debt scare or actual loss; a bank requiring insured receivables before increasing a trade finance line; a credit manager frustrated by unexplained credit limit cuts from their direct insurer; a CFO who just saw a competitor go under after a buyer insolvency
- **Pull** (what attracts them to Lockton): Independent advocacy at claims; access to multiple insurers vs being locked into one; a broker who actually knows Asia trade credit risk, not a generic insurance generalist
- **Habit** (what keeps them stuck): "We've had the same Coface policy for 5 years and it's fine" — renewal inertia; the finance team doesn't want another vendor relationship to manage
- **Anxiety** (switching worries): Will there be a gap in cover during transition? Will the new insurer know our buyers? Will switching cost more?

## 9. Customer Language

- **How they describe their problem (verbatim):**
  > "Our biggest buyer is in China and honestly if they don't pay one quarter we're in trouble."
  > "Our bank keeps asking us about our receivables exposure and I don't have a good answer."
  > "We had a customer go under two years ago and we wrote off nearly $800K. Never again."
  > "Our insurer just cut the credit limit on one of our top buyers and won't tell us why."
  > "We're trying to grow into Vietnam and India but we don't know the buyers there."

- **How they describe a good solution:**
  > "Someone who actually picks up the phone when something goes wrong."
  > "We want a policy that actually pays out, not one where they find a reason to reject the claim."
  > "If our credit limit gets cut, I want someone who can challenge that."

- **Words to use:** receivables, buyer risk, credit limit, open account, bad debt, cash flow protection, policy advocacy, claims support, credit exposure, trade terms
- **Words to avoid:** "peace of mind," "comprehensive coverage," "protect your business," "synergy," "leverage," "best-in-class," "holistic"
- **Product-specific terminology:**
  - TCI = Trade Credit Insurance
  - Credit limit = the maximum insured exposure on a single buyer approved by the insurer
  - Protracted default = buyer hasn't paid but hasn't formally gone insolvent (typically 6–9 months overdue)
  - Whole turnover policy = covers all buyers across the receivables book (most common structure)
  - Named buyer / key accounts policy = covers only specified high-value buyers
  - Euler Hermes / Allianz Trade, Coface, Atradius = the three dominant global TCI insurers
